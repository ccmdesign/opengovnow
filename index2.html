What is Open Government?

Open Government benefits us all. With increased government transparency and citizen participation, public services are more efficient and actually serve the citizens who need them most.

Open Governments are built on two things: information and collaboration. A government that is open releases information about budgeting, contracting, spending, and aid to be freely open and available to the public, in a way that average citizens can understand. Equally important: an Open Government is one that actively invites citizens to be participants in government decision-making. This two-way relationship between citizens and governments is the foundation of Open Government.

The Open Government agenda makes sure that governments and citizens actually work together to achieve better outcomes for all. No one group of people has the solutions to the most pressing governance challenges, and that’s why openness and collaboration are essential. If citizens and governments work together, we can all co-create practical solutions to long-standing problems.

Who Wants It?

Felipe Estefan

Job Position with a few words a few more

Interview clip from Felipe Estefan about the importance to measure citizen demand

Citizens overwhelmingly want Open Government. In October 2013, The Global Opening Government Survey was conducted that asked citizens, among others, about how open they perceive their government to be. 61% of respondents said they want their government to be more open. In some countries, there is an overwhelmingly high demand from the younger generation. For example in Mexico, more than 83% of the respondents aged 18-25 advocate for a more open government. In a separate survey by the UN and its partners, “an honest and responsive government” is among the Top 3 priorities (out of 16 topics) of citizens surveyed around the world.

The Global Opening Government Survey was conducted as a response to the growing demand to better understand citizens’ views on the current state and the potential impact of openness. Using the innovative “random domain intercept” technology, the survey is based on a brief questionnaire and has collected complete responses from over 65,000 individuals in the first 61 OGP member countries plus India.*

Eric Meerkamper

Job Position with a few words a few more

Interview clip from Eric Meerkamper on RDIT technology/survey methodology

The survey results provide benchmark evidence on three main areas: (i) citizens' perception on how open their governments are; (ii) the demand for increased openness and the impact this would have on citizens' lives; (iii) the potentials for increased openness, i.e., increased tax revenues, higher degrees of trust, etc. These results have the potential to inform the ongoing international efforts to explore and examine the connection between openness and effective governance.
